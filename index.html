The value of Blood

    Blood has many functions, but transfusion medicine addresses mainly the role of blood in delivery of oxygen throughout the body and control of bleeding at sites of injury. Red Blood Cells (RBCs) meet the body’s demand for oxygen by carrying it from the lungs to the tissues. If the supply of oxygen drops too quickly, or too low as in severe anemia and blood loss, the body is unable to compensate and symptoms such as low blood pressure, weakness, faintness, tissue damage and death may result. RBC transfusion improves the supply of oxygen to critical tissues and can prevent these complications. Platelets and plasma are transfused to support blood clotting and prevent bleeding for patients ranging from those undergoing cancer chemotherapy, bone marrow and stem cell transplants, to trauma victims and those with liver disease.

Blood Groups

    There are 4 main blood groups defined by the ABO system: blood group A – has A antigens on the red blood cells with anti-B antibodies in the plasma blood group B – has B antigens with anti-A antibodies in the plasma blood group O – has no antigens, but both anti-A and anti-B antibodies in the plasma blood group AB – has both A and B antigens, but no antibodies Blood group O is the most rare blood group.
